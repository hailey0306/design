UI Assets Redesign

Do Good Points is a start-up company that I am working at right now. This page discusses how I redesigned the some important UI assets on our website as a UI/UX designer. After these smalls changes, our website's bounce rate decreased by 13% and the completes rate increased by 5%.

BACK TO TOP

Overview

Design in business.

After we launched our new Do Good Points landing page and user flow, we partnered Affiliate Marketing Platforms to buy more traffic to our site. The traffic started to pick up slowly, but the data showed that people were not donating from our site, so we investigated the problem, and decided to improve our design and build more trust from our users.

I was part of the ambitious project to redesign the Discover Wall to make the call.

To comply with my non-disclosure agreement, I have omitted confidential information in this case study. All information in this case study is my own and does not necessarily reflect the views of Do Good Points.
Background

Asking for donations — it’s intimidating. We get it.

Do Good Points is the first of its kind rewards community. We are not a non-profit who ask for money directly. We want people to come to our site to explore the organizations that they like then make the decision. But why they should come to our site?

1. Discover.

There are more than 1.5 million non-profit organizations registered in the U.S. We want people to come to our site to explore charities and get general information so that they can choose which charity they wish to donate to.

2. Reward.

For every donation that people make from our site, they can earn points for rewards.

3. Record.

We know this question will come during tax season: “Did you make any charitable contributions?” Of course. Did you document that donation? Sigh. Maybe not well enough. Even though we do not make documentation of your donations, we can keep records of the different organizations you donated to so you can track the amounts you paid out to the charities.

USER PROBLEM

Based on what we have on the journey map, here we summarized three main user problems.

  • Users feel confused about how the points system works;

  • There is not enough charities information for users to explore and build trust;

  • Everything looks fine but no attractive points to donate.
Analysis & Solution

1. How can we get people involved in discover?

For our users to explore different charities, we have a discover wall that lists all the charities we are partnering with. Each charity represented in a small tile including a promo image and the basic introduction in the text labels so that our users have a general idea about the charity.

Here were the changes we made:

1. Image

We used to have a square-sized image on the top part of the tile. We realized if we cropped the image to make it shorter, we would have a wider space for other components.

2. Title

Instead of just placing the charity name, we used “donate to” because we wanted people to make the action. However, the short sentence can make the tile look busier and the word “donate” might intimidate our users. So, we decided to change them back to just the title of the charity.

3. Charity Details

We wrote an abbreviate content below the title if the users want to see more information. However, this unfinished sentence didn’t make any sense. So, we changed them to a short tagline to engage people to discover more information.

4. Points

Originally, we told people how many points they would get per dollar that they donate, “Earn seven points per dollar.” But seven points looks and sounds small, and it’s not attractive enough. Some people may be unable to calculate the total amount. Instead, we use the total points they would get per donation.

We invited a few people to test our website in a church (A study shows religious individuals are more likely to donate to charity). They liked our concept, but when they saw the charity tile, they didn’t feel there was a clear connection between the value and the points. So, we came up with the idea to create “marketing tile” to educate people how much value our points would have.

2. What do people want to see before donating?

To redesign the expanded view (the modal window after the user clicks "view charity"), I did the desk research and interviewed a few people to understand what people wanted to know before donating and what kind of charities they donate to. I also realized that people might not understand what “Charity Navigator” is. And people might be interested but still hesitate to donate.

DESIGN DECISIONS

  • Change the image to a carousel/slider to show more images and video and drive their emotion;

  • Add the spending ratio to show more related data;

  • Add an info icon and the hover effect to introduce “Charity Navigator.”

  • Add a "Save for Later" button allows users to make the decision later.

TESTING RESULT

  • 4/10 of them didn’t understand what the “Program Expenses” and “Accountability & Transparency Score” were;

  • 6/10 thought if they click "donate now," there would be a payment interface appearing.

After the first round, we did some testing. We found that people didn’t understand what the “Program Expenses” and “Accountability & Transparency Score” meant. I explained to them that “Program Expense” means the percentage of the charity's total expenses spent on the programs and services it delivers. Some people would still ask, “where is the rest of the money?” even though 89.3% is a very good Program Expenses percentage. Even though save for later is a good feature for users, our marketing team members thought it shouldn't be a button.

ITERATION

  • Keep the basic introduction, but add the categories;

  • added charity logo to increase authenticity and make charity more trustable;

  • Rather than using "donate now" as a call to action button, Use "donate to them directly" to educate our users that they are not donating to us;

  • Change the "Save for Later" button to text.

Complement

People are attracted by “Gift Cards”.

To improve the overall user experience, I kept testing and iterating this project. One thing I noticed is that our users are attracted by "Gift Cards." They ask questions like, "how can I redeem?" Not just our user, when I was talking about our product, I mentioned "Gift Cards," people around came and asked, "I heard 'Gift Cards,' how can I get one?"

Also, according toBlackhawk Network’s State of Consumer Gift Card Preferences in 2018, 55% of consumers surveyed report being interested in giving or receiving digital gift cards that can be added to a mobile app or digital wallet, especially Millenials.

What makes us different is we rewarding our users after they donated. I redesigned the "Redeem Page" as well because I believe a better redemption experience can incentivize a user to donate.

Conclusion

Continuous Iteration, Not Preparation

I know that our current website is not perfect, but we are trying to improve the overall experience day by day. After these smalls changes, our website's bounce rate decreased by 13% and completes rate increased by 5%.

I was surprised that a recent survey shows that over 40% of the companies still aren’t talking to their end users during development. However, even though we are a small early-stage startup company, we speak to our end-users. We de-risk development by continually listening, testing, and iterating with end-users.

Note: Because the business owner has changed, this project has been assigned to another designer right now.
